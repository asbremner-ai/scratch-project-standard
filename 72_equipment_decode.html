Decoding the
Marketing

MOI, CG, forgiveness, AI-designed faces, low-compression "distance" balls — the equipment industry runs on jargon that is designed to sell, not to inform. This guide translates the buzzwords into what they actually mean for your game, and gives a tiered, current buying guide for the launch monitors and rangefinders worth your money. It complements the fitting guides (15 and 55) but is aimed at the shop-floor decision, not the fitting bay.

Marketing vs. Reality

Every year the marketing promises more distance and more forgiveness. The rules of golf cap both — which tells you that most headline gains are either tiny, or come from fitting rather than from the technology itself.

"The single biggest gains in your bag come from getting the clubs you already own fitted to you — not from the newest driver on the wall."

The fitting-first principle
The Ceiling
Why "More Distance" Is Mostly Capped

The Rules Limit the Very Things Being Advertised

Driver face springiness (measured as "characteristic time," limited to 239 microseconds), the overall distance a conforming ball may travel, and the resistance of a driver head to twisting (moment of inertia, capped for competition heads) are all regulated. Manufacturers are already at or near these limits. So when an advert promises "15 more yards," be sceptical: for most golfers the real yardage on the table comes from the right loft, the right shaft, and centred contact — not from this year's face technology.

The Honest Framing
What Technology Genuinely Does

Consistency, Not Miracles

Modern equipment is genuinely better — but the benefit is mostly consistency on mishits, not extra distance on your best strike. A forgiving driver does not make your centre-face bomb go further; it makes your heel strike lose less. That is a real and valuable benefit — just not the one the headline number is selling.

Club Jargon, Translated

The five terms below appear on nearly every club advert. Here is what each actually controls.

TermWhat it literally isWhat it means for you
MOI (Moment of Inertia)Resistance of the head to twisting on off-centre hitsThe real meaning of "forgiveness." Higher MOI = the face stays squarer and holds ball speed on heel/toe strikes. Matters most for higher handicaps and off-centre misses.
CG (Centre of Gravity)The balance point of the headLow and back = higher launch, more spin, more forgiveness. Low and forward = lower spin, more workable, less forgiving. Adjustable weights simply move the CG around.
ForgivenessMarketing umbrella termReally means retained ball speed and tighter dispersion on mishits — a product of high MOI and face design. Not a single measurable number, so treat the word itself with caution.
Face technologyVariable-thickness / AI-designed / flash facesAims to keep ball speed high across more of the face, not just the centre. Genuinely useful, but capped by the springiness limit — the gain is uniformity, not raw speed.
Offset / bounce / lie / loftReal, measurable geometryThese change ball flight far more reliably than any "tech." Correct loft and lie, and appropriate wedge bounce for your turf and strike, are worth more than any marketing feature.
The One That Matters Most

Adjustability Is a Fitting Tool, Not a Gimmick

The adjustable hosel sleeve on modern drivers and fairways lets you change loft, and often lie and face angle, by a degree or two. This is genuinely useful — it can straighten a persistent slice or add launch — but only if it is set correctly for you. Bought off the shelf and left on the default setting, it does nothing. If you own an adjustable club, getting it dialled in is free yardage you have already paid for. See Guide 15 (Equipment Fitting).

Ball Jargon, Translated

The golf ball is the only piece of equipment you use on every shot, and the most misunderstood. Ignore the distance headlines — fit the ball from the green backwards.

Compression

Mostly Feel, Not Free Distance

Compression describes how much the ball deforms at impact. Lower-compression balls feel softer and can help players with slower swing speeds feel they are compressing the ball — but the idea that a "soft" ball is automatically longer is largely a myth. For most golfers the distance difference between compressions at their own speed is small. Choose compression for the feel you prefer, then judge the ball on its greenside spin and flight, not its softness rating.

Construction & Cover

Where the Real Difference Lives

TypeCoverBest for
2-piece (distance)Ionomer / SurlynDurability, straighter flight, lower spin, lower price. Good for high handicappers who lose balls and want value.
3–5-piece (tour)UrethaneLow driver spin for distance plus high wedge spin for greenside control — the layers separate the two. For players who can benefit from stopping the ball on approach shots.

The cover is the headline. Urethane gives the soft feel and high greenside spin tour players want; ionomer (Surlyn) is more durable, lower-spinning and cheaper. If you can control your wedges enough to want spin, a urethane ball is where your money should go before any new club.

The Claim to Ignore

"The Longest Ball"

Every premium ball is within a rules-mandated distance ceiling and within a yard or two of its rivals at a given speed. "The longest ball" is a testing-condition claim, not a meaningful advantage. Pick your ball on greenside spin and flight, buy it by the dozen, and — crucially — play the same model every round so your distances stay consistent.

The Device Buying Guide

Personal launch monitors and rangefinders have transformed at the affordable end of the market. Below is a tiered guide to what is worth buying, by budget and purpose. Prices are UK RRPs at the time of writing (2026) and will move — treat them as a guide, not gospel.

Launch Monitors — Measuring Your Strike
Entry · around £199

Shot Scope LM1 — Your First Launch Monitor

A Doppler-radar unit that sits behind the ball and shows five core numbers — club speed, ball speed, smash factor, carry and total distance — on its own 3.5-inch screen, with no phone and no subscription. It does not measure spin or launch angle, but for building an honest set of carry distances and tracking speed training it is superb value and the easiest way into launch data. The natural companion to Guide 22 (Wedge Distances) and Guide 25 (Speed Training).

Mid · around £470–£500

Garmin Approach R10 — The All-Rounder

A radar unit delivering ten-plus data points including estimated spin, plus a home-simulator mode with virtual courses. Portable, long battery life, phone-app based. Some advanced features sit behind a subscription. The sensible choice if you want more than raw distances — shot shape, launch, and light simulator use — without moving to premium money.

Upper consumer · around £650–£700

Rapsodo MLM2PRO — Measured Spin & Video

Adds dual high-speed cameras on top of radar, so it captures genuinely measured spin (rather than estimated), plus real impact and swing video. Uses marked premium balls for its most accurate spin numbers. The pick if you are serious about indoor practice, want trustworthy spin data, and will use it as a simulator. Beyond this tier you are into professional-grade units (Trackman, GCQuad) at many times the price — rarely justified for a personal buyer.

Choosing a Distance Device

Laser vs. GPS vs. Hybrid

TypeStrengthWatch-out
Laser rangefinderPin-precise to the flag; fast; no subscriptionNeeds a clear line of sight; you must aim it steadily
GPS (watch or handheld)Front/middle/back and hazard numbers at a glance; no aimingDistances to the green centre, not the actual pin

Slope warning: a rangefinder with slope (adjusting yardage for uphill/downhill) is not permitted in most competitions. Any device you use in a qualifying event must have its slope function switched off or physically disabled — most competition-legal models have a visible toggle. See Guide 12 (Rules of Golf).

A Different Tool Again

Shot-Tracking Systems (Arccos, Shot Scope)

Screw-in sensors or a tag-based system that record every shot you hit on the course and build strokes-gained-style statistics over time. This is not a launch monitor and not a rangefinder — it answers "where am I actually losing shots?" rather than "how far is this one?" If your goal is data-driven improvement, this category matters more than a new club. See Guide 26 (Stats Interpretation).

Shop-Floor Tactics

How to walk into a pro shop or big-box store and not be sold the wrong thing.

  • 1
    Never buy on the feel of one good hit. A single flushed shot with a demo club tells you almost nothing. Ask to hit ten balls and to see the launch-monitor averages — carry, dispersion and ball speed — not the best number of the session.
  • 2
    Compare against your current club, on the same monitor, the same day. The only meaningful question is "is this better than what I already own, for me?" A new club that gains you nothing over your gamer is not worth the money, however good the marketing.
  • 3
    Beware the stock shaft. Off-the-shelf clubs come with a default shaft that suits a narrow band of golfers. The head gets the marketing budget, but the shaft often decides your result. A "worse" head with the right shaft can beat a "better" head with the wrong one.
  • 4
    Last year's model is this year's bargain. Because real year-on-year gains are small, the previous generation — heavily discounted once the new range lands — is usually the smartest value in the shop. You are paying for the fitting and the shaft, not the paint job.
  • 5
    The device is not the fitting. A launch monitor tells you numbers; it does not tell you which club is right without someone who can interpret them. For a genuine club change, get a proper fitting (Guides 15 and 55). For understanding your own game between fittings, a personal launch monitor is the tool.

The Short Version

Five Takeaways

Spend Where It Actually Counts

  • 1
    Distance is capped by the rules — most headline yardage claims are marketing. Real yardage comes from fitting and centred contact.
  • 2
    "Forgiveness" means high MOI — it protects your mishits, it does not lengthen your best strike.
  • 3
    Fit the ball from the green backwards — pick urethane for spin and control if you can use it; ignore "the longest ball" claims.
  • 4
    A £199 radar launch monitor now gives most golfers everything they need to build honest carry distances — the biggest bang-for-buck device on the market.
  • 5
    Switch off slope in competition, and never confuse a launch monitor, a rangefinder and a shot-tracker — they answer three different questions.
