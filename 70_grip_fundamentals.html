Strike Pillar · Guide 70

Grip
Fundamentals

The grip is your only physical connection to the club — and the single biggest influence on clubface angle, which controls roughly three-quarters of where the ball starts. This guide covers the full-swing grip (strong, neutral, weak), the three ways to join your hands, and the putting grips that fix the most common stroke faults. Grip pressure has its own guide (62); this one is about hand position and shape.

🤝 Hand Position🎯 Face Control⛳ Putting Grips🔧 Fault Fixes

Why the Grip Governs Ball Flight

Modern launch data changed how we understand ball flight. The clubface — not the swing path — is the dominant factor in where the ball starts and a major factor in how it curves. And the face is controlled, more than anything else, by the hands.

"Good golf begins with a good grip."

Ben Hogan — the first fundamental of Five Lessons
The Ball-Flight Law
Face vs. Path

The Face Starts the Ball, and Your Hands Own the Face

Under the modern ball-flight model, the ball starts roughly 75–85% in the direction the clubface is pointing at impact, and curves away from the swing path relative to that face. This means a slice, hook, push or pull is very often a face problem — and the face is set, first and foremost, by where your hands sit on the club. Fix the grip and you often fix the flight without touching the swing itself.

The Cost of a Bad Grip
Compensations

A Poor Grip Forces the Body to Compensate

If the grip delivers a face that is chronically open or closed, the golfer unconsciously builds compensating moves — flipping the hands, holding off the release, stalling the body — to square it up in time. These compensations must be re-timed on every swing, which is why grip-driven ball flights are so inconsistent under pressure. A sound grip removes the need to compensate, and is the cheapest, fastest improvement available to most amateurs.

Strong, Neutral & Weak

"Strong" and "weak" describe rotation, not effort. They refer to how far your hands are turned around the grip — which sets the tendency of the clubface to close or open through impact. This is the setting that changes ball flight independent of your swing path.

How to Read Your Own Grip

Knuckles & the "V"s

Take your normal grip and look down. Count the knuckles you can see on your lead hand (left hand for a right-hander), and note where the "V" formed by thumb and forefinger of each hand points. These two checkpoints tell you instantly whether your grip is strong, neutral or weak.

The Practical Point
Grip as a Cure

Match the Grip to Your Miss

Fighting a slice? Strengthen the grip — rotate both hands slightly away from the target until you see a third knuckle. Fighting a hook? Weaken it toward neutral. Change one increment at a time (a quarter-turn), hit ten balls, and read the result. Most amateurs slice, and most amateurs hold the club too weak — the single most common grip fault in the game.

Palm vs. Fingers

Where the Handle Sits in the Lead Hand

Independent of strength, the club should run across the base of the fingers of the lead hand, not up through the palm. A grip seated in the fingers lets the wrists hinge freely — which is where clubhead speed comes from. A grip buried in the palm restricts the hinge and leaks power. Check: when you close the lead hand, the pad of the hand should sit on top of the grip, and you should feel the handle under the fingers, not in the palm.

Joining the Hands: Overlap, Interlock, Ten-Finger

This is a separate decision from strong/neutral/weak. How you connect the two hands affects how unified they feel and where power comes from — but it does not, on its own, change the face angle. Pick the one that lets your hands work as a single unit.

Vardon / Overlap

The Most Common Choice

The trail-hand little finger rests on top of, or in the channel between, the lead-hand index and middle fingers. Named after Harry Vardon, it unifies the hands and is the default for most players with average-to-large hands and reasonable hand strength. If in doubt, start here.

Interlock

Locked Together

The trail-hand little finger and lead-hand index finger interlock. It creates a more connected, "welded" feel and suits players with smaller hands, shorter fingers, or less grip strength — it stops the hands separating. Both Jack Nicklaus and Tiger Woods used it. The trade-off: it can encourage a slightly weaker lead-hand position if not monitored.

Ten-Finger / Baseball

All Ten on the Club

Every finger sits on the grip with no overlap or interlock. It gives the trail hand maximum leverage and is genuinely useful for juniors, players with small or weak hands, seniors, and anyone with arthritis or hand-joint pain. The watch-out is trail-hand dominance — the stronger hand can take over and flip the face closed. If you use it, keep the hand pressures matched.

📋 Quick Test

Which Join Suits You?

Grip the club each way and make three slow half-swings with each. The correct one for you is whichever lets both hands feel like a single unit with no gap opening at the top of the backswing — not the one a particular tour player uses. Hand size and strength decide this, not fashion.

Putting Grips — Fixing the Stroke at the Source

The full-swing grip is about power and face control. The putting grip is about the opposite: removing the small muscles and wrist action that cause misses. Each putting grip below solves a specific problem — the skill is matching the grip to your fault, not copying a favourite pro.

The Baseline

Conventional Reverse Overlap

Lead-hand index finger lies over the fingers of the trail hand (the reverse of the full-swing overlap). Palms face each other so the hands work together and the wrists stay quiet. It is the standard, feel-friendly starting point and works well for players whose stroke is already reliable. If your putting is not broken, there is no need to change from this.

Fixes: pulls, wristy strokes, breakdown

Cross-Hand (Left-Hand-Low)

The lead hand goes below the trail hand on the grip. This levels the shoulders at address, quiets the lead wrist, and stops it "breaking down" (flipping) through impact — the cause of pulled putts and inconsistent pace. It promotes a stroke driven by the rocking of the shoulders rather than the hands. If you pull short putts or feel handsy over the ball, this is the first change to try. Widely used on tour; Jordan Spieth is a well-known example.

Fixes: yips, pushes, deceleration, pressure twitch

The Claw (Pencil / Saw)

The lead hand holds the grip conventionally at the top; the trail hand is turned so it holds the putter lightly like a pencil or claw, off to the side. This largely removes the trail hand's small muscles from the stroke — the muscles responsible for the yips and for pushing or flicking the face. It stabilises the face beautifully under pressure. If you have a nervous, twitchy trail hand or the yips, the claw is the classic answer. Used by Justin Rose, Sergio Garcia, and (in his own variant) Phil Mickelson.

Fixes: everything wrist-related — the maximum-stability option

Arm-Lock

The grip is pressed against the lead forearm and the shaft leans forward, effectively taking the wrists out of the stroke entirely. This is legal under the anchoring ban as long as the club is held against the forearm and not anchored to the body. It delivers the most stable face of any method, but requires a purpose-built arm-lock putter — longer, with more loft and a stronger shaft lean built in. Bryson DeChambeau, Matt Kuchar and Webb Simpson are notable users. Consider it if you have tried everything else and still lack a repeatable stroke.

Choosing Your Grip — A Decision Framework

Grip is not a matter of copying; it is a matter of matching your grip to your body and your misses. Work through the three decisions in order, changing one thing at a time.

  • 1
    Set the strength to fight your miss. Slice or push-fade → strengthen toward three knuckles. Hook or pull → weaken toward one. No consistent curve → neutral, two knuckles. This is the setting that changes your ball flight, so change it first and in quarter-turns.
  • 2
    Pick the join by hand size and strength. Average/large hands, decent strength → overlap. Small hands, short fingers, weaker grip → interlock. Very weak hands, juniors, arthritis → ten-finger. This does not affect flight, so choose purely on which feels unified.
  • 3
    Match the putting grip to your stroke fault, using the table in the previous tab. Only change from conventional if you have an actual, repeating miss to solve.
  • 4
    Give any change four rounds before judging it. A new grip always feels wrong for the first two sessions because your hands have a strong memory. Feel is a liar here; ball flight and made-putt count are the only honest referees. Cross-reference Guide 62 — Grip Pressure & Tension for how hard to hold once the position is right.
For Every Handicap

Where This Pays Off Most

For a 24-handicapper, correcting a chronically weak, slice-inducing grip is often worth several shots a round on its own — it is the highest-leverage change available. For a low-handicap or scratch player, the grip is usually already sound, so the value shifts to the putting grip: switching to a claw or cross-hand under competitive pressure can be the difference between holing and missing inside ten feet when it matters.

Checking & Grooving Your Grip

The grip degrades slowly and invisibly — it drifts a little every few weeks until, months later, a fault has appeared "from nowhere." These checks keep it honest.

🪞 Drill 1 — The Mirror & Photo Check

See What You Actually Do

Once a month, take your grip and photograph it from face-on and down-the-line, or check it in a mirror. Count the lead-hand knuckles and check the "V"s against the tab above. Golfers are astonishingly bad at feeling their own grip — the camera does not lie, and this two-minute check catches drift before it becomes a swing fault.

🎯 Drill 2 — Split-Grip Face Awareness

Feel the Face Rotate

Grip the club with your hands slightly apart (an inch of gap). Make slow half-swings and feel how the trail hand rotates the face open and closed through impact. This exaggerates the sensation of the hands controlling the face and teaches you what "square" feels like. Then rejoin the hands and keep the same feeling.

⛳ Drill 3 — The Putting Grip Ladder

Prove Which Grip Wins for You

On the practice green, hit ten putts from six feet with each grip you are considering (conventional, cross-hand, claw). Count makes. Repeat over three sessions. The grip with the highest total make-count is your grip — this replaces guesswork and pro-copying with your own evidence.

Reminder

One Change at a Time

Never alter grip strength, join, and putting grip in the same week — you will not know which change did what. Isolate one variable, test it over four rounds, keep it or bin it, then move to the next. Grip changes are the most powerful and the most patience-testing improvements in golf.
