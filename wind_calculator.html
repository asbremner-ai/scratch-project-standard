The Algorithm

How Wind Affects Carry Distance

The adjustments in this calculator are based on the Broadie/PGA Tour carry data model, calibrated for the typical amateur ball speed range (140–160 mph ball speed) rather than Tour data. The key principle: a headwind's effect on carry is roughly twice as large as a tailwind's benefit at the same speed — because headwind also increases spin rate.

Headwind Rule
Into the Wind

1 Club per 10 mph (adjusted for trajectory)

The standard amateur rule of thumb is one extra club per 10 mph of headwind. This calculator refines that based on ball flight:

  • ↑
    High ball flight: +1.5 clubs per 10 mph. Higher flights expose more surface area to the headwind and generate more spin — the effect compounds significantly above 20 mph.
  • →
    Mid ball flight: +1 club per 10 mph. The standard rule applies. 15 mph headwind adds approximately 15 yards of effective distance.
  • ↓
    Low ball flight: +0.7 clubs per 10 mph. A knockdown or punch shot is markedly less affected by headwind — this is why the shot is invaluable in UK conditions.
Tailwind Rule
Downwind

Roughly Half the Headwind Effect

Tailwind reduces the required club, but the benefit is smaller than the headwind penalty at the same speed. A 20 mph tailwind saves approximately 1 club, not 2. This asymmetry is consistent across ball-flight research and is why downwind approaches are often easier than they appear — less distance to cover, but the difference is smaller than intuition suggests.

Crosswind Rule
Left and Right Wind

Aim Adjustment, Not Yardage Adjustment

Crosswinds affect direction more than carry distance. The calculator provides aim compensation in yards (lateral drift at the target) rather than a club change:

  • 1
    For a 150-yard shot in 15 mph crosswind: aim approximately 10–15 yards into the wind. The ball drifts back to the centre.
  • 2
    Always aim to the upwind side of the green — not directly at the flag. Let the wind bring the ball to centre.
  • 3
    Diagonal winds (e.g. into-left) have a combined effect: treat the into component for yardage, the left component for aim.
Important Caveat
Calibrate to Yourself

These Are Starting Points — Not Absolutes

Wind effect varies with swing speed, ball type, and individual shot shape. Use this calculator to create a starting decision, then track your results in your practice journal (Guide 17). After 10 rounds of logged wind shots, you will know whether you are an "over-club" or "under-club" player in wind. Adjust the output of this tool accordingly.

Links Specific

Why Links Wind Needs Different Thinking

Links wind behaves differently to parkland wind for three reasons: there are no trees to shelter approach shots; firmer ground means run-out varies unpredictably with wind direction; and coastal wind gusts are typically stronger and more variable than inland conditions.

  • 1
    Add 20% to the calculator output when on exposed links. The lack of tree shelter means the effective wind speed at ball height is higher than at ground level — where you felt it at address.
  • 2
    Downwind on links: always consider running the ball. A tailwind + firm fairways means drives travel significantly further. More importantly, downwind approach shots land hotter — use the bump-and-run as the primary shot rather than trying to stop the ball.
  • 3
    Into-wind approach: always favour long. Short is dead on links. Every pot bunker, swale, and collection area is in front of the green. A long approach on links is almost always manageable. Short is penalised structurally by the course design.
Ground Game Integration
Bump and Run in Wind

When to Abandon the Aerial Game Entirely

In winds of 20 mph or above on links or heathland courses, the bump-and-run removes wind as a variable entirely — particularly for shots from 80 yards and in. The low trajectory means:

  • →
    Headwind has minimal effect on a 7-iron bump from 50 yards — the ball is in the air for less than a second.
  • →
    Crosswind cannot push a ball running along the ground. All lateral aim adjustments become irrelevant.
  • →
    The uncertainty of aerial distance control in strong wind is eliminated — the carry variable disappears.

See Guide 50 — Links Golf Strategy for the complete bump-and-run technique system and club selection framework.

Links Mental Discipline
Accepting the Wind

The Cognitive Shift That Scores

The largest links wind error is not technical — it is psychological. Players who resist the wind by swinging harder, fighting crosswinds, or attacking pins in conditions that do not allow it typically score 4–6 shots worse than their ability. The player who accepts that 20 mph is a feature of today's course — not an obstacle — adjusts targets, plays for centre-green, and trusts the bump-and-run, scores close to their handicap in conditions that defeat others. Use this calculator to make objective decisions before emotion intervenes.
