Starting Golf:
Kit, Costs & First Steps

What to buy, what to skip, where to play and the first-month plan — the honest version, without the £2,000 shopping list. Golf is cheaper and friendlier to start than its reputation suggests.

What Golf Actually Costs to Start

The industry will happily sell a beginner £2,000 of equipment. You need a fraction of that — and spending less at the start is genuinely better for your golf, because your swing will change enormously in the first two years.

The Honest Starter Budget

£150–350 Covers Everything

A used half-set (driver, a couple of hybrids or fairway woods, 7/9 irons, wedge, putter) from Facebook Marketplace, eBay or a pro shop part-exchange rack costs £80–200 and is all any new golfer needs. Add a bag (£20–50 used), a glove, a dozen mid-price balls and comfortable trainers-with-grip or entry golf shoes, and you are playing.

Where to Play

You Do Not Need a Membership Yet

Municipal and pay-and-play courses cost £10–25 a round, par-3 and pitch-and-putt courses less, and driving ranges £5–8 a bucket. Nine holes after work on a quiet course is the ideal learning environment. Club membership makes sense once you play twice a week or want a handicap and competitions — Guide b5 covers that decision properly.

Lessons vs Self-Taught

The One Purchase That Outperforms Everything

If the budget allows one luxury, make it three beginner lessons with a PGA professional (£30–50 each, often cheaper in blocks or groups) rather than better equipment. Grip, posture and ball position set in the first month will either help or haunt you for a decade — they are near-impossible to self-diagnose and trivial for a coach to set correctly. Group beginner courses (6 weeks, ~£60–100) are outstanding value and add the social side.

Your First Month, Week by Week

The goal of month one is not a score. It is repeatable contact, basic etiquette confidence, and the habit loop of regular short practice.

Weeks 1–2

Range and Practice Green Only

  • 1Book lesson one; tell the pro you want grip, posture, ball position and one contact drill.
  • 2Two range visits: 50 balls maximum, 8-iron only, tee the ball if it helps. Quality over volume.
  • 3Two putting-green visits: roll putts to different holes — free, and putting is a quarter of all shots.
  • 4Watch pace-of-play basics (Guide b4) before your first course visit.
Weeks 3–4

First Nine Holes

  • 1Pick a quiet par-3 or short course, late afternoon midweek.
  • 2Play with a more experienced friend if possible — say you are new; golfers are almost universally kind to beginners.
  • 3Scoring rule: pick the ball up once you have hit 3 over par on any hole. Nobody counts. Keep moving.
  • 4Note what actually went wrong (usually contact, not direction) — that is next week’s range focus.
The Only Rule That Matters Now

Contact Before Everything

Direction, distance and score are all downstream of striking the ball before the ground. Guide b2 is the complete method. If a range session has one purpose for your first three months, it is this.
