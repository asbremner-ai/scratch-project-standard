The Stats Framework

On-course stat tracking converts subjective "I think I need to work on X" into objective "the data shows I lose 1.2 strokes per round on approaches from 150–175 yards." The difference between those two statements is the difference between purposeful practice and comfortable practice.

📊 Data-Driven Improvement
Why Traditional Stats Miss the Point

Fairways Hit and GIR Are the Wrong Metrics

Traditional golf statistics — fairways hit, greens in regulation, putts per round — are flawed as improvement guides. They conflate quality and context. Strokes Gained solves both problems by measuring performance relative to expectation — every shot compared to the Tour average for that exact situation.

Why Traditional Stats Mislead
Putts per round: 28 putts could mean excellent putting
OR missing 8 greens by 1 foot (easy chips, short putts).
Same number — completely different meaning.

GIR: 10 greens hit from average of 185 yards
OR 10 greens hit from average of 142 yards.
Same GIR — completely different ball-striking quality.

Strokes Gained — Deep Dive

Each SG category measures a distinct part of the game and requires a different interpretation approach. Understanding what the numbers mean — and what they don't — prevents misdiagnosis and misallocated practice time.

🔬 SG Mechanics
SG: Approach — The Most Impactful Category

Where Most of the Game Is Won or Lost

  • 1
    What it measures: The expected strokes to hole out from your approach position compared to where you finish. If you are 150 yards out (expected 2.9 strokes to hole) and finish 25 feet from the flag (expected 2.1 strokes) — you gained 0.8 SG on the approach.
  • 2
    Distance bands matter: Break SG: APP by distance band — 100–125, 125–150, 150–175, 175–200, 200+. A player who loses 0.3 SG from 150–175 yards but gains 0.1 SG from 125–150 has a very specific practice target. The overall number hides this.
  • 3
    Direction of miss: If 68% of short-side misses are left, that tendency is systematic — adjust aim or check shot shape consistency.

Critical cross-reference: Poor SG: PUTT can indicate approach shot problems, not putting problems. SG: Off the Tee

Distance vs. Accuracy Trade-off

  • 1
    Distance vs. accuracy trade-off: SG: OTT captures both. A short accurate drive and a long slightly offline drive can produce the same SG value if expected strokes from each position are equal.
  • 2
    Penalty frequency first: OB, water, and unplayable lies are the most damaging tee shot outcomes. One penalty shot per round costs more SG than any other single tee shot metric. Address penalties before addressing distance.
  • 3
    Average approach distance bridge: If adding 20 yards of driving distance reduces average approach from 168 to 148 yards, the GIR gain from that distance increase is quantifiable — and is often the strongest argument for speed training.

Arccos & Shot Scope

Both platforms automatically track every shot using GPS sensors. Both produce SG data but differ in interface, subscription model, and depth of specific analytics. Arccos now offers three hardware options including Air (sensorless) and Gen 4 sensors.

📡 Platform Guide
Arccos — Gen 4 Sensors & Air

Three Hardware Options, Same Analytics

  • 1
    Arccos Air (sensorless — recommended if already using): Tracks shots using only the Link Pro device in your pocket — no sensors on clubs required. GPS-based shot detection powered by a firmware update to the existing Link Pro. If you already have a Link Pro, the update is free. Less precise than sensor-based tracking on short shots and putting, but excellent for full-swing data and SG: OTT and SG: APP.
  • 2
    Gen 4 Smart Sensors (launched early 2025): 16 sensors (14 + 2 spares), improved connectivity, refreshed 2D/3D course mapping with pinch-to-zoom, and a new AI Rangefinder. The Smart Club Distances feature has been significantly improved — your actual carry per club from real tracked rounds, updated continuously. Most accurate option for all SG categories including putting.
  • 3
    Strokes Gained dashboard: The home screen shows SG breakdown across all five categories with trend lines. Navigate to Analytics → Strokes Gained → Rolling Average. The 10-round rolling average is the only number that matters for practice decisions.
  • 4
    Approach analysis by distance: Under Analytics → Approach, filter by distance band. Identify your worst-performing 25-yard window. This data point should determine the next 4 weeks of approach practice.
  • 5
    Caddie AI: Following Caddie recommendations has been shown in Arccos internal data to improve approach proximity vs. manual club selection for many players. Use it for 10 rounds to evaluate its impact on your own game before deciding whether to rely on it. Note: AI Caddie must be disabled for competition — check your club's local rules before the round.
Shot Scope V5 Pro

Key Features and Dashboard Navigation

  • 1
    Performance dashboard: Shot Scope compares your stats to your handicap group rather than Tour average — arguably more useful for practice prioritisation than Tour benchmarking. The V5 Pro (current model, released 2024) adds a larger display and enhanced shot detection accuracy. The companion app has expanded its SG analytics significantly since 2024, now offering full five-category SG breakdowns directly on the platform.
  • 2
    Proximity to hole by distance: Navigate to Approach → Proximity → by Distance. Your average distance from the flag by 10-yard distance band — the clearest single view of approach shot performance. Shot Scope's handicap-group comparison means you see where you underperform vs. similar-handicap players, not just Tour.
  • 3
    Miss pattern mapping: Shot Scope plots every approach miss on a green graphic. Over 20+ rounds, this reveals a clear directional bias. If 65% of misses are left, that is your systematic miss — adjust your aim point (see Guide 20 Approach Miss Direction).
  • 4
    Phone-free advantage: Shot Scope tracks automatically via the wrist device without a phone on the course — a meaningful benefit for those who prefer a clean, undistracted round. Data syncs to the app post-round. The wrist GPS also doubles as a rangefinder with hazard distances on approach shots.
Golfmetrics

Manual-Entry SG — The Precision Tool

  • 1
    What makes Golfmetrics different: Unlike Arccos and Shot Scope (GPS auto-tracking), Golfmetrics is manual-entry. You input each shot's lie category, distance, and outcome. This requires discipline but produces more accurate data — GPS tracking misses nuances like unplayable lies, gimme putts, and penalty drops that Golfmetrics captures precisely.
  • 2
    The SG workflow: Navigate to Stats → Strokes Gained. Golfmetrics uses a comprehensive shot-value database to calculate SG for every lie category at every distance. The sub-category depth — SG: Approach broken down by lie (fairway, rough, bunker, recovery) and distance band — is more granular than Arccos or Shot Scope.
  • 3
    Best for: Players who prefer accuracy over convenience. The data quality advantage compounds over time. Widely used by serious UK county-level amateurs. Annual subscription approximately £50.
  • 4
    Key report — GIR by lie: Golfmetrics shows GIR percentage broken down by lie type (fairway, rough depth, specific areas). This reveals whether approach problems are distance-based, lie-based, or directional — a distinction GPS platforms cannot make cleanly.

Strokes Gained Without a Tracking Device

Every recommendation in this system assumes SG data — but the methodology does not require Arccos, Shot Scope, or any paid tracker. A pen, a simple scorecard template, and 10 minutes per round produces genuinely useful SG estimates. This tab is your complete starting point if you have no tracking device, or simply prefer manual recording.

📝 The Five-Number Method
What to Record — Five Numbers Per Hole

The Minimum Viable Dataset

  • 1
    Tee shot result (par 4s/5s only): Fairway hit (F), missed left (L), missed right (R), or penalty (P). One letter, no detail needed.
  • 2
    Approach proximity: After your approach shot to the green (the shot that's meant to find the green), estimate distance to the hole in paces (1 pace ≈ 1 yard). "12 paces" is good enough — you don't need a laser.
  • 3
    Greens in regulation: Y or N. Did you reach the green in the "regulation" number of shots (2 for par 4, 1 for par 3, 3 for par 5)?
  • 4
    Putts taken: The number of putts on that hole. This single number, tracked over 20 rounds, tells you more about your putting than almost any other metric.
  • 5
    Up-and-down (if applicable): If you missed the green, did you get up and down (chip/pitch + 1 putt or fewer)? Y or N.
💡

Where to write it: Most scorecards have a blank column or back page. A 5-character code per hole (e.g. "F-12-Y-2-—") takes under 10 seconds to record after each hole and does not slow play.

The Spreadsheet Option

Free Manual SG Tracking — Google Sheets

If you want closer-to-true SG numbers without a paid tracker, a free Google Sheet with the five inputs above plus a lookup table of PGA Tour average "expected strokes to hole out" by distance and lie (publicly available from Mark Broadie's published research and multiple golf analytics blogs) lets you calculate genuine SG values manually.

  • ▸
    The calculation: SG for a shot = (Expected strokes from your starting position) − (Expected strokes from your ending position) − 1. For example, an approach from 150 yards in the fairway has an expected value of roughly 2.8 strokes. If your shot finishes 10 feet from the hole (expected value ≈ 1.5), your SG for that shot = 2.8 − 1.5 − 1 = +0.3.
  • ▸
    Realistic expectations: This takes longer than wearing Arccos sensors — roughly 15–20 minutes of data entry per round. For players who enjoy the analytical process, it's genuinely viable. For players who find it tedious, the five-number method above gives 80% of the practical value for 10% of the effort.
  • ▸
    When to upgrade: If after 10–15 rounds of manual tracking you're finding real value in the data, Arccos Air (Guide EP8) removes the recording burden entirely and gives you automatic SG by category, shot-by-shot. The manual method is an excellent way to confirm the system is worth the investment before buying hardware.
⚠️

Don't let perfect be the enemy of useful: Manually-tracked data is noisier than sensor data — a single round tells you very little. The five-number method becomes useful after 10 rounds and genuinely informative after 20, exactly like Arccos. The advantage of starting now with manual tracking is that you begin building your 20-round sample immediately, at zero cost, while you decide whether to invest in hardware.

Prioritising Improvements

Your SG data reveals multiple weaknesses simultaneously. The skill is identifying which weakness to address first — the one that produces the largest expected scoring improvement per hour of practice invested.

⭐

The 10 HCP to scratch truth: Across 200,000+ tracked rounds, SG: Approach accounts for 40% of the scoring gap between 10 HCP and scratch. Approach before putting — always. The instinct to practise putting is strong; the data consistently shows approach work pays more.

When to Deviate From Data Priority

Conditions Where the Matrix Does Not Apply

  • 1
    Technical change in progress: If you are currently rebuilding a swing element (e.g. wrist mechanics), that work takes priority regardless of what the SG data says. The technical fix is the root cause; the SG loss is the symptom. Fix the cause.
  • 2
    Small sample size: If your data is based on fewer than 15 rounds, do not make major practice plan changes. See the Sample Size tab for minimum sample thresholds.
  • 3
    Competition-specific weakness: If you have an important competition on a specific type of course (links, parkland, hilly) in 4 weeks, practise the skills that course demands — even if they are not your statistically weakest category.

Data to Practice Plan

Translating SG data into a practice plan requires a clear bridge between what the numbers show and what specific drill or session addresses it. This tab provides that bridge.

📋 Translation Framework
SG Data → Specific Practice Action

The Four-Step Translation

  • 1
    Identify the category: Which of the four SG categories shows the largest negative relative to target? Check your 10-round rolling average, not a single round.
  • 2
    Break down to sub-skill: Within SG: Approach, is the loss concentrated at 150–175 yards, or 125–150? Within SG: Putting, is it lag putting or short putts? The sub-skill is the drill target.
  • 3
    Diagnose the cause: Is the loss a technical issue (use Mevo and HackMotion), a strategic issue (use miss pattern data), or a practice-to-course transfer issue (use the Practice Structure Science guide)?
  • 4
    Set a measurable target: "Improve SG: Approach from 150–175 yards by 0.3 over the next 6 weeks" is measurable. "Work on my irons" is not. Every practice block should have a specific SG sub-category target with a timeframe.

Sample Size & False Patterns

The greatest risk in data-driven improvement is making significant practice changes based on patterns that do not yet exist — small samples amplify noise into apparent signals. Understanding minimum sample requirements prevents wasted practice time chasing ghosts.

Common False Pattern Traps

What Looks Like Signal But Is Noise

  • 1
    "My putting has got worse" after 3 rounds: Three rounds of poor putting could be random variance. The standard deviation of SG: PUTT across rounds is approximately ±1.0 — meaning a normally distributed putting performance can look like +1.0 in round 1 and −1.2 in round 2 with no real change in skill. Never change your putting approach based on 3 rounds.
  • 2
    Course difficulty masking SG categories: Playing a hard course will produce worse absolute SG numbers but is not evidence of skill decline. Compare SG across rounds on similar-difficulty courses, or use handicap-adjusted SG (Shot Scope provides this).
  • 3
    Weather effects on SG: OTT: Wet conditions reduce driving distance and increase dispersion. A run of wet-weather rounds will produce artificially low SG: OTT that corrects automatically in dry conditions.

Weekly Multi-Tool Synthesis

Your programme uses five data sources simultaneously: Arccos/Shot Scope (on-course SG), Mevo (ball flight and club data), HackMotion (wrist mechanics), video analysis (movement pattern), and HRV/readiness (training state). Each guide covers its tool in isolation. This section provides the unified weekly review workflow that synthesises all five into a single, coherent practice priority each week.

🗓️ The 30-Minute Weekly Review Protocol
Sunday Evening Review — Complete Step-by-Step

30 Minutes That Set the Week's Practice Priority

  • 1
    Step 1 — SG Review (5 minutes): Open Arccos or Shot Scope. Check the 10-round rolling SG across all four categories. Note which category shows the largest gap from your target. Note whether it is improving, stable, or worsening compared to last week. Write down the weakest category and the sub-skill within it (distance band, shot type).
  • 2
    Step 2 — Mevo Data Review (8 minutes): Open your Mevo session data from the past week's range sessions. Check the specific metrics that correspond to your SG weakness:
    • SG: APP weak → check attack angle, face angle, F2P consistency, smash factor
    • SG: OTT weak → check club speed trend, spin axis, smash factor
    • SG: ARG weak → check wedge spin rates, attack angle on short shots
    • SG: PUTT weak → Mevo does not measure putting — skip to HackMotion
    Note whether Mevo confirms the SG weakness (e.g. high spin axis = confirming face control issue) or contradicts it (e.g. Mevo looks good but SG: APP still weak → strategic/course-management cause, not technical).
  • 3
    Step 3 — HackMotion Review (7 minutes): Open HackMotion session data. Check the wrist position benchmarks for your primary Mevo finding:
    • High spin axis + open face angle → check lead wrist extension at top and impact
    • Low smash factor + early release → check radial deviation at top (lag loading)
    • High shot-to-shot variance → check address position consistency (P1 repeatability)
    Compare this week's readings to your benchmark session. Note if the metric is moving in the right direction, has plateaued, or has regressed.
  • 4
    Step 4 — HRV Trend Review (3 minutes): Open your HRV app. Check your 7-day average vs. your 30-day baseline. If HRV is trending down across the week, your training load may be too high — reduce volume next week. If HRV is above baseline, you are well recovered — push intensity next week on speed and strength sessions.
  • 5
    Step 5 — Weekly Priority Decision (5 minutes): Based on steps 1–4, write down one primary practice focus for the week. Use the format:
    "Priority: [specific skill] using [specific drill] because [SG data says X] confirmed by [Mevo/HackMotion says Y]."
    Example: "Priority: Lead wrist flexion at impact using HackMotion biofeedback because SG: APP is −0.9/round with high spin axis (avg ±14°) confirmed by HackMotion showing +8° lead wrist extension at P7."
  • 6
    Step 6 — Update Progress Journal (2 minutes): Log the priority and supporting data in Guide 17 (Progress Journal). Note Mevo benchmarks and HackMotion positions from this week. This creates the longitudinal record that reveals real progress vs. noise over months.
