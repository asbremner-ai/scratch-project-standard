Your Week

Structure Beats Volume Every Time

Saturday is designated as your competitive or pressure round — medal, stableford, or a money match. Sunday practice addresses what the data from the week revealed. Never practise mechanics on the day you play.

Practice Session Design

How Each Session Must Be Structured

  • 1SG-led warm-up (10 min): Identify from your most recent round data which category was worst. Begin with that category.
  • 2Deliberate practice block (30 min): One skill, specific feedback, progressive challenge. Track proximity or make rates — not time on task.
  • 3Pressure block (10 min): Same skill under consequence — putting for money, chipping to a tight target with a score to beat. Non-optional.
  • 4Transfer block (10 min): Play the skill in a game-like format — 9-hole short game simulation, shots from real lies. Checks whether practice has transferred.
The Rule

Never Practise Your Strengths Under Time Pressure

The most common practice mistake is spending 60% of session time on the range hitting drivers. Your SG data will almost certainly show that your off-tee category is not your largest gap. Practise your weakest SG category first, every session — until the data says otherwise. Check your data →

Physical Preparation

PREPARATION

TPI research shows 80% of swing faults have a direct physical cause. Strength, mobility, aerobic capacity, and on-course nutrition are performance levers that directly affect your SG data.

💪 Strength Training — 2× Per Week
The Core Programme

Hip Hinge, Rotation, Anti-Rotation Core

These three movement patterns directly translate to swing efficiency, clubhead speed, and consistency under fatigue.

  • A1Hip hinge (deadlifts / RDLs): 3 sets × 6–8 reps. Develops posterior chain power that drives sequencing and clubhead speed.
  • A2Hip thrusts / glute bridges: 3 sets × 10–12 reps. Glute strength predicts lateral stability. Weak glutes = early extension = fat shots.
  • B1Cable wood chops (rotational): 3 sets × 10 each side. Trains rotational power delivery at golf-specific speeds.
  • B2Pallof press (anti-rotation): 3 sets × 10 each side. Core stiffness that maintains posture through impact.
  • C1Goblet squats: 3 sets × 10 reps. Hip mobility under load — directly improves stance stability.
  • C2Single-leg deadlifts: 2 sets × 8 each side. Balance and glute recruitment under unilateral load.
Cardiovascular Training — 2–3× Per Week

Back-Nine Fitness: The Overlooked Killer

Amateur scores rise an average of 0.4 strokes per hole on holes 14–18 due to fatigue-driven cognitive decline. Scratch golfers sustain decision quality and physical precision for the full 18.

  • ►Protocol: 30–40 minutes at zone 2 (conversational pace). Run, cycle, row, or brisk walk. Never the day before a competitive round.
  • ►Target: Resting heart rate below 60 bpm within 3 months. Correlates directly with sustained cognitive performance across a 4-hour round.
  • ►Walk the course: 18 holes walking ≈ 8km of zone-2 cardio. Two walks per week is your full cardio requirement.

Speed Development — Rypstick Protocol

Every 1 mph Adds 2.5 Yards of Carry

For players with driver speed below 95 mph, adding 5 mph through overspeed training adds 12–15 yards — often the difference between a 6-iron and an 8-iron approach. Speed is trainable at any age.

  • ►Protocol: Rypstick overspeed system, 3× per week, 10 minutes per session. Warm-up 5 reps → lag training 10 reps → max speed 15 reps. 4–8% speed gains in 4–6 weeks.
  • ►Timing: Begin once short game and putting foundations are in place. Speed without directional control first is counterproductive.
  • ►Sleep: Eight hours is not optional. Sleep deprivation reduces fine motor precision 20–30% and decision quality 25%.
🍎 Nutrition Protocol
Pre-Round — 2 Hours Before Tee

The Foundation Meal

  • ✓Ideal: Oats with berries and eggs / wholegrain toast with eggs and avocado / chicken and rice. Protein + complex carb + healthy fat. Medium portion.
  • ✓Hydration: 500ml water in the hour before the round. Even mild dehydration (1–2%) measurably impairs fine motor precision.
  • ✓Caffeine: 100–200mg, 45 minutes before the round. More than 300mg causes tremor — directly impairs putting. No caffeine after the 12th hole.

Recovery Nutrition

What You Eat After Affects How You Train Tomorrow

  • ►Within 30 minutes post-round: 20–30g protein + 40–60g carbohydrate. A protein shake with a banana is sufficient.
  • ►Evening before competition: Higher-carbohydrate dinner with lean protein. Avoid alcohol — even one unit impairs next-day fine motor precision by a measurable margin.
  • ►Sleep: 8 hours is the target. Sleep below 6 hours reduces fine motor precision 20–30% — equivalent to playing mildly drunk.
💧

The simplest win in this entire plan: Drink 500ml of water before every round and eat something on holes 6 and 12. Most amateur golfers play the back nine mildly dehydrated with falling blood glucose. Fixing this alone is worth 0.5–1.0 strokes per round.

Competitive Exposure

YOU MUST COMPETE

This is the most commonly missed element in a self-directed programme. Your handicap will not accurately reflect your ability until you perform consistently under real competitive pressure. Pressure is a skill — and it must be trained.

“Practice puts your brain in a state of controlled comfort. Competition puts it in a state of controlled discomfort. You need both — but scratch golfers have learned to perform in the second state.” — Dr. Gio Valiante, sports psychologist to Dustin Johnson
Competition Requirements

Your Competition Targets

    Mental Game Under Pressure

    The Three Routines That Must Hold

    • 1Pre-shot routine without deviation: Same sequence, same duration (20–28 seconds), same trigger, every shot. Routine consistency is the #1 predictor of performance under pressure. If your routine changes when nervous, it is not yet a routine.
    • 210-second bad shot rule: Allow 10 seconds of honest reaction to a bad shot. After that, the door closes. Any longer than 15 seconds of rumination measurably impairs the next shot.
    • 3Process goals only on the course: Outcome goals (“I need to birdie this hole”) trigger anxiety that disrupts automatic execution. Process goals (“I’m committing fully to this routine”) keep attention where performance lives.
    Competitive Self-Talk

    The Language You Use Changes What You Do

    Research by Dr. Antonis Hatzigeorgiadis shows that instructional self-talk (“smooth tempo,” “stay down”) improves execution of skilled motor tasks by 12–18% versus silence. Negative self-talk (“don’t hit it in the water”) impairs performance by 15–22% — your brain processes the image of the feared outcome. Build a vocabulary of positive, process-focused cue words for each shot type.
