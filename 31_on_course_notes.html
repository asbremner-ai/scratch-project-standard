Course Knowledge as Competitive Advantage

Tour caddies spend up to 40 hours building course notes before a tournament. Scratch amateur golfers who play their home course 30+ times a year often have no written notes at all — relying on memory that degrades, distorts, and fails precisely when precision is most needed: under competitive pressure.

📋 The Knowledge Compound
Why Written Notes Beat Memory Every Time

The Four Failure Modes of Memory-Based Course Knowledge

  • 1
    Pressure degrades recall: The specific details you most need under competitive pressure — the exact carry to clear the bunker on 14, whether the green slopes left or right at the back — are precisely the details that become uncertain under cortisol-driven arousal. Memory and pressure are incompatible. Written notes are immune to pressure.
  • 2
    Memory updates overwrite correct data: A single bad experience on a hole — misjudging the wind once — can update your mental model of that hole incorrectly. Your notes record measured reality. Your memory records recent experience, which is not the same thing.
  • 3
    Seasonal changes are invisible without records: Summer carry distances, winter run-out, wet green stopping power — all vary by 10–30% across the year. Without documented seasonal baselines, you are re-learning the same information every season instead of referencing it.
  • 4
    Course improvements are lost between seasons: Your notes improve every time you add to them. After 20 rounds with structured notes, your course intelligence is substantially more accurate and complete than it was after 5. Without notes, 20 rounds produces roughly the same memory-based knowledge as 5.

⭐

The caddie standard: A tour caddie's yardage book is a working document — annotated, dog-eared, and updated after every practice round. It is the physical embodiment of accumulated course intelligence. Your digital equivalent should function the same way. The goal is not a perfect document — it is a living record that gets more accurate with every round.

The Recon Protocol

Course reconnaissance is not just walking the course — it is a structured data-gathering exercise with specific objectives. Done properly, a single dedicated recon round produces more useful course intelligence than 20 casual rounds without a framework.

🔍 The Reconnaissance Round
When to Do a Dedicated Recon Round

Four Triggers for Structured Reconnaissance

  • 1
    New course before a competition: Any course you are playing competitively that you have not played in the current conditions deserves a dedicated recon round. Even if you have played it 10 times, conditions change. A recon round the day before confirms current carry distances, green speeds, and pin position areas.
  • 2
    Your home course at the start of each season: Winter conditions (soft greens, limited run, different landing zones) versus summer conditions (firm ground, maximum run, different club requirements) are sufficiently different to warrant a fresh recon at the start of each season. Your summer notes are not valid for winter golf.
  • 3
    After course alterations: New bunkering, tree removal, bunker positions, green renovations — any structural change invalidates specific sections of your existing notes. A targeted recon of the affected holes updates the record.
  • 4
    When your scoring on specific holes is consistently worse than expected: If your SG data (Guide 26) shows you losing significantly more strokes than expected on 2–3 specific holes across multiple rounds, those holes require dedicated recon. The problem is usually a course knowledge gap — a hazard carry you are misjudging, a green slope you are misreading.

The Recon Round Methodology

A recon round is played differently from a scoring round. You are collecting data, not competing. Bring a notebook or phone, play slowly, and measure everything.

Tee Area — What to Measure and Record

The Eight Data Points From Every Tee

  • 1
    GPS or laser measurement to all significant hazards: Carry distance to bunkers (front lip, not centre), water carries (minimum safe carry, not optimal), trees that require carry or navigation. Record the exact GPS number from the tee markers — not from a position halfway up the fairway. Tee distances are the ones you will use in competition.
  • 2
    Ideal landing zone boundaries: Where does the fairway widen? Where does it narrow? Where does a specific miss (left, right, short) go from bad to catastrophic? These boundaries define your target — not just the centre of the fairway.
  • 3
    Slope of the fairway at the landing zone: Does the fairway slope left, right, toward the green, or away from it at 230–260 yards? A fairway sloping left at the landing zone turns a left-centre tee shot into a worse position than a right-centre shot, regardless of where you aimed.
  • 4
    Wind funnel note: Does the hole create a wind tunnel effect? Are there trees that redirect or accelerate the wind? Note the prevailing wind direction on this hole and whether it differs from the general wind direction — hills, valleys, and tree corridors commonly produce local wind effects 20–30° different from the forecast direction.
  • 5
    Elevation change to landing zone: Does the tee sit above or below the fairway? A 10m elevation drop adds approximately 10% to effective carry (the ball flies further before landing). An elevated target (uphill hole) reduces effective carry by a similar amount. Note the elevation change and its approximate effect on required club.
  • 6
    Scoring category — personal: Based on your game and historical data, is this a scoring hole (opportunity), a neutral hole (par is fine), or a danger hole (bogey avoidance priority)? This classification determines your strategic approach before you leave the tee — not on the fairway where you can see the trouble.
  • 7
    Preferred miss direction: Which miss position is less damaging — left rough or right rough, short or long of the green? Identify the recoverable miss and aim so that your dispersion cone covers more of the recoverable zone than the dangerous one.
  • 8
    Firm conditions vs. soft conditions tee plan: In summer (firm, running), a shorter club may produce the same or better position due to roll-out. In winter (soft, no roll), carry-dependent distances change the club required. Document both conditions separately.
Approach Zone — What to Measure

The Most Valuable Recon Data

  • 1
    GPS distance from ideal landing zone to flag: Walk to your ideal landing zone and record the GPS distance to the front, centre, and back of the green. Most players only record centre-of-green distance — but the front-to-back difference on approach clubs (often 15–25 yards) is the difference between hitting the green and missing short.
  • 2
    Best approach angle: Some greens are better attacked from the left of the fairway; some from the right. A green that slopes from right to left is accessed best from the right side of the fairway (putting downhill) or the left (putting into the slope — harder but safer). Note which approach angle gives you the most green to work with and the most forgiving miss.
  • 3
    Green surrounds — the miss priorities: Walk around the green and assess: which miss is worst (steep bank, tight lie, deep bunker, water)? Which miss is most recoverable (flat fringe, open chip angle, friendly rough)? The approach target should be set so that the edge of your dispersion cone falls into the recoverable miss zone, not the catastrophic one.
  • 4
    Carry to clear any front bunkers: Front bunkers are the most punishing approach hazard — they combine the difficulty of a bunker shot with a short approach. Measure the carry required from your expected distance to clear the front lip, not just the bunker face. A ball that lands in the back of the bunker is as costly as one in the front.

Hole Note Structure

The optimal hole note captures the essential data in a format that can be read quickly under competitive pressure. Every field earns its place — if it doesn't change a decision, it doesn't belong in the note.

Scoring Category Classification

Classify Every Hole Before You Play It

  • S
    Scoring hole: Your game gives you a genuine birdie or net birdie opportunity. Risk-reward justifies aggressive play. Attack accessible flags. Accept slightly higher variance for higher reward. Typically 3–5 holes per 18.
  • N
    Neutral hole: Par (or net par) is the correct target. No meaningful scoring opportunity. No exceptional danger. Play conservatively, execute your standard process. Typically 8–10 holes per 18.
  • D
    Danger hole: Significant risk of a costly score. Bogey avoidance is the primary objective. Conservative off the tee, centre-green on approach, lag putting. Never attack sucker pins. Typically 3–5 holes per 18.
💡

Personalise your classification: The stroke index tells you what the committee thinks is difficult. Your personal classification tells you what is difficult for your game. A long par-4 (SI 1) might be neutral for a player hitting 270-yard drives but a danger hole for a 240-yard player. Classify based on your actual game, not the SI.

Personal History — The Most Valuable Field

Building a Statistical Profile of Every Hole

  • 1
    Record your score after every round: A simple running total of your scores on each hole, updated after every round. After 10 rounds, your average score per hole is your most honest assessment of where strokes are actually going — more reliable than any single-round memory.
  • 2
    Note the cause of every bogey or worse: Not "I played badly" — the specific cause. "Drove right into bunker." "3-putted from above the hole." "Tried to carry the bunker, came up short." After 5+ instances on the same hole, patterns emerge that directly inform your strategy note.
  • 3
    Record any shot that worked exceptionally well: The specific club, wind direction, and ball flight that produced your best result on a hole. This positive evidence is as valuable as the negative — it tells you the shot that works when it goes right, which is what you should be visualising in the pre-shot routine.

Green Maps

Putting is the category where course knowledge provides the clearest, most immediate advantage. A player who knows the slope direction, the approximate gradient, the grain, and the speed variation of every green on their home course has a measurable putting advantage over a player who reads it fresh every time.

⛳ The Green Intelligence System
Building a Green Map

The Four Elements of a Complete Green Profile

  • 1
    Primary slope direction: Every green has a dominant slope — the direction water would run if poured on the surface. This is your macro read. Note the primary slope direction for each quadrant of the green (front-left, front-right, back-left, back-right). Most greens slope from back to front (toward the fairway) and from one side — often toward a nearby water feature or natural drainage point.
  • 2
    Gradient estimate: How steeply does the green slope? Greens are typically graded in three levels: gentle (less than 1%), moderate (1–2%), and steep (over 2%). A gentle slope requires a small break read. A steep slope on a fast green can require 4–5 feet of break on a 20-footer. Categorising each quadrant by gradient avoids the systematic under-reading of subtle slopes and over-reading of flat areas that costs most amateurs strokes.
  • 3
    Grain direction: Relevant primarily on UK links courses and bent/bermuda grass greens common in warmer climates. Grain runs in a consistent direction — typically toward the prevailing wind, toward the setting sun, or toward nearby water. Putting with the grain: ball rolls faster and breaks less. Against grain: slower, breaks more. On bentgrass parkland greens, grain effect is usually minimal — note if it is significant on specific greens.
  • 4
    Speed variation across the green: Some greens are significantly faster in the back half than the front (common on elevated greens where the back falls away sharply). Others are fast on the perimeter and slower in the centre (hollow areas). If you have consistent 3-putt patterns from specific areas, a speed variation in that zone is likely the cause.
Quadrant Analysis — The Practical System

Dividing Every Green Into Zones

Rather than trying to read every individual putt fresh, map each green into four quadrants and record the dominant read from each zone. This gives you a baseline that is almost always faster to consult than re-reading from scratch.

ZoneRecordHow to Capture
Front-leftSlope direction + gradient + typical break on 20-footerWalk to zone during recon, observe drainage direction
Front-rightSlope direction + gradient + typical break on 20-footerNote during recon; update after each missed read
Back-leftSlope direction + gradient + typical break on 20-footerOften the fastest quadrant — note any speed difference
Back-rightSlope direction + gradient + typical break on 20-footerNote whether the back falls away from the green (common)
💡

The confirmation loop: After every round, note any putt where the actual break differed significantly from your map prediction. Update the map. After 10+ rounds, your green maps will be accurate to the point where you rarely misread your home course by more than 10–15% — a significant advantage over a fresh read every time.

Pin Position Intelligence

The Most Tactically Useful Green Data

  • 1
    Categorise every flag position as accessible or dangerous: An accessible flag is one where the approach angle and surrounding hazards allow you to attack with acceptable risk. A dangerous flag (sucker pin) requires approach precision that exceeds your realistic dispersion — it is only worth attacking when you need a birdie to win something specific.
  • 2
    Note the required approach angle for each pin area: The left-back flag might require an approach from the right side of the fairway for an acceptable angle. The right-front flag might be attackable from anywhere. This approach-angle intelligence is the link between your tee strategy and your pin position plan — and it can only be built through deliberate observation.
  • 3
    Record the "never go" zones: For every green, identify the one area that makes the subsequent shot longest and most difficult. "Never above the hole on 12." "Never right on 4 — the bank rejects everything to the rough." These are non-negotiables that should be in your hole note as unconditional rules.

Seasonal Adjustments

Golf conditions change dramatically across a season in any temperate climate. A course knowledge system calibrated to summer is significantly wrong for winter. Documenting seasonal baselines allows you to adjust automatically rather than re-learning the same lessons every winter.

🌦️ Seasonal Adjustment Framework
The Four Playing Seasons — Temperate Climate

SpringMar–MayRecovering, variable, unpredictableMost variable season — measure rather than assume; greens speed up rapidly in April
Documenting Seasonal Distance Changes

Your Personal Summer vs. Winter Distance Table

The most practical seasonal data you can build is your personal carry + total distance table for each club in summer conditions versus winter conditions. Most players know they lose distance in winter — very few know exactly how much per club, which causes systematic under-clubbing.

  • 1
    Measure in September and March: Use the Mevo (or Arccos real-world data) to establish your carry distances in early autumn (before significant softening) and early spring (before ground firms up). These two measurements bracket your winter distance loss and give you exact adjustment figures.
  • 2
    Record total distance (carry + run) separately from carry: In summer, a 7-iron that carries 155 yards might total 170 yards with run. In winter, that same 7-iron carries 150 yards and totals 152 yards. The 18-yard total distance difference requires a club and a half adjustment — significantly more than most players budget for.
  • 3
    Update the caddie card seasonally: Your caddie card (Guide — Caddie Reference Card) should have separate summer and winter distance matrices. If you are using a single matrix year-round, you are using the wrong numbers for at least 5 months of the year.
Seasonal Hole Strategy Changes

Which Holes Change Most Between Seasons

  • 1
    Long par-4s and par-5s: These change most dramatically. A reachable par-5 in summer (driver + long iron) may become a three-shot hole in winter (driver + fairway metal + approach). The strategy note for these holes must have explicit summer and winter variants — the decision tree at the tee is completely different.
  • 2
    Carry-over-water holes: In summer, your normal carry clears water hazards comfortably. In winter, reduced ball compression and cold air (approximately −1 yard per °C below 20°C) can make previously comfortable carries marginal. Note any water carries within 5% of your normal summer carry — these become club-up decisions in winter.
  • 3
    Elevated or exposed holes: Holes on high ground or exposed to prevailing wind experience greater temperature and wind variation between seasons. These holes are disproportionately affected by winter conditions and may require 2–3 clubs more in a cold headwind compared to a summer tailwind — a 5–6 club swing that completely changes the strategic approach.

Digital Setup

Paper notes have the advantage of being available on the course without a phone. Digital notes have the advantage of being searchable, updatable, and shareable. The optimal system uses both — digital as the master record, a printed or PDF summary as the on-course reference.

📱 Tools & Workflow
Recommended Digital Tools

The Two-Layer System

  • 1
    Notion (Master Record): Create a database with one entry per hole. Each entry has all eight fields from the hole note template, plus a photo of the green map, and a rolling score history. The database view allows you to filter by scoring category (see all danger holes at once), by recent updates, or by holes where your score is below target. Link it from the Progress Journal (Guide 17) as your course intelligence hub.
  • 2
    Notes app + screenshots (On-Course Reference): Before a competition round, screenshot the key pages of your Notion database and save to your phone's camera roll. You now have all hole notes available offline, without requiring connectivity on the course. This takes 3 minutes before the round.
  • 3
    GPS app annotation (Arccos/Shot Scope): Both Arccos and Shot Scope allow custom notes on holes. Use these for the 2–3 most important data points per hole — the ones that change decisions on every visit. Full notes stay in Notion; decision-critical notes go in the GPS app.
  • 4
    Video walkthroughs (optional): For complex greens, a 60-second video walkthrough during a recon round — narrating the slope, the speed variation, and the approach angle — is searchable and more memorable than text alone. Store in the Notion entry alongside the written notes.
The Update Workflow

Keeping Notes Current Without It Becoming a Burden

  • 1
    Voice notes during the round: After a hole that revealed new course intelligence — a putt that broke more than your map predicted, a landing zone that was firmer than noted — record a 10-second voice note on your phone. Transcribe to Notion that evening. This is faster than writing mid-round and captures the insight while it is fresh.
  • 2
    Post-round debrief → notes update (Guide 08): As part of the post-round debrief, spend 3–5 minutes updating any hole notes that the round revealed were incorrect or incomplete. This becomes automatic after a few rounds and takes very little time.
  • 3
    Seasonal refresh: At the start of summer and winter, review the notes for the 5 holes that changed most. Update distance data, ground condition notes, and tee strategy where conditions have made the previous notes inaccurate. This takes 20–30 minutes twice a year.
