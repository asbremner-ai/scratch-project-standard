Friendly Games &
Money Matches

Match play, medal and Stableford have their own guides — this one is about the games you actually play on a Saturday morning fourball: Nassau, skins, wolf, bingo-bango-bongo and the rest. How each works, how to handicap them so they are fair across abilities, and how to agree and settle a bet without any awkwardness.

Why a Small Bet Improves Your Golf

A modest wager is not really about the money. It is about creating consequences — a mild, friendly pressure that makes every shot matter and turns a casual round into useful practice for competition.

"A game is much more fun — and much better practice — when there is a little something riding on it. You learn how you behave when a shot counts."

Common wisdom of the weekend fourball
The Real Value
Pressure Rehearsal

A Bet Is the Cheapest Pressure Training There Is

The four-foot putt to win a hole for £2 rehearses exactly the nervous system you must control in a club medal or county qualifier. Players who habitually play for a little something arrive at genuine competition already used to a shot mattering. It is the same reason the pressure-round guides exist — a friendly game builds the same skill, for free, every week.

The One Rule That Matters
Agree Everything on the First Tee

Settle the Format, Stakes and Strokes Before You Play

Every dispute in a money game comes from something that was not agreed before the round. Before the first tee shot, agree three things out loud: the game, the stake, and how handicap strokes are applied. Then there is nothing to argue about at the 18th. If anyone is unsure or uncomfortable with the stake, lower it — the point is enjoyment, never anyone's wallet.

The Classics — Nassau, Skins & Match Bets

These three cover the vast majority of friendly golf. Learn them and you can join almost any game.

The Nassau

Three Bets in One Round

The most popular wager in golf. A Nassau is really three separate matches for equal stakes: the front nine, the back nine, and the full eighteen. A "£2 Nassau" means £2 for each — a maximum of £6 changes hands. Because the back nine and the overall are still alive after a poor front nine, no one is ever out of it.

  • →
    The press. When a player or side falls two down in any of the three matches, they may "press" — starting a brand-new side bet for the same stake over the remaining holes. Presses can be optional or automatic; agree which on the first tee. They keep a runaway match interesting but can multiply the stakes, so know what you are agreeing to.
Skins

Every Hole Has a Prize

Each hole is worth one "skin." To win the skin you must have the outright best score on that hole — if two or more players tie, no one wins it. Tied skins usually carry over to the next hole, so a hard-fought hole late in the round can be worth several skins at once. Skins works brilliantly for three or four players of any mix of abilities.

  • →
    Validation option: some groups require a "validation" — win a skin and you must at least tie the next hole to bank it. Optional, adds spice, agree beforehand.
Straight Match & Medal Bets

The Simplest Wagers

A match-play bet is simply a stake on the result of a hole-by-hole match — win, lose or halve, settled on the last hole played. A medal (stroke-play) bet settles on total strokes, best used when everyone wants a single, simple wager on the round. Both are ideal for two players; the Nassau is the three-in-one upgrade of the match bet.

Team & Group Games

These shine in a fourball, and several are deliberately designed so a higher-handicapper can win points, keeping mixed-ability groups genuinely competitive.

Wolf

The Rotating Captain

A four-player game. On each tee, one player is the "Wolf," in a fixed rotation. The Wolf tees off last. As each of the other three players drives, the Wolf must decide straight away — before the next player hits — whether to take that player as a partner for the hole (two-versus-two). Pass on all three and the Wolf is committed to going it alone. If the Wolf fancies the odds, they can declare "Lone Wolf" before anyone tees off and play one-against-three for double or triple points. High risk, high reward, and endlessly entertaining.

Bingo-Bango-Bongo

Three Points a Hole — Rewards Every Ability

Three points are available on every hole, and crucially they reward order of play, not just the best golfer:

PointAwarded to
BingoFirst ball on the green
BangoClosest to the pin once all balls are on the green
BongoFirst ball into the hole

Because points depend on order (furthest from the hole always plays first), a shorter or higher-handicap player can win Bingo by being first on, or Bongo by holing first. It is the fairest game for a mixed group and needs no handicap adjustment at all.

Vegas

Pairs, Scores Combined Into a Number

Two-versus-two. Each pair combines their scores on a hole into a two-digit number, lowest first — a 4 and a 5 become 45, not 9. The difference between the two teams' numbers is the points won. Birdies can "flip" the opponents' number (a 6 and 4 becoming 64 instead of 46), so the swings get large. A lively game for confident groups — keep the per-point stake tiny.

Quick-Fire Add-Ons

Side Games Layered on Top

  • →
    Snake: whoever three-putts holds the "snake" and owes the pot — until someone else three-putts and takes it. The player holding it at the 18th pays out. Cruel and very effective at sharpening lag putting.
  • →
    Sixes (Round Robin): in a fourball, partners rotate every six holes so everyone partners everyone else across the round. Fair and sociable.
  • →
    Aces & Eights / greenies & sandies: small bonus points for a hole-in-one attempt closest ("greenie" on par 3s), or getting up-and-down from a bunker ("sandie"). Layer sparingly.

Handicapping the Bet — Making It Fair

A game between players of different abilities is only fun if the strokes are fair. This is the part most casual groups get wrong. Here is how to do it properly.

The Core Method

Play Off the Lowest Handicapper

  • 1
    Find the lowest handicap in the group and set them to zero. Everyone else receives the difference between their course handicap and the lowest player's. A 20 playing a 6 receives 14 strokes; the 6 receives none.
  • 2
    Allocate those strokes by the card's stroke index. The 14-shot player gets a stroke on the 14 hardest holes (stroke index 1 through 14). On stroke-index-1 holes they may get one; if anyone is owed more than 18, they get a second stroke starting again at index 1.
  • 3
    A net birdie beats a gross par. On a hole where the higher-handicapper receives a stroke, their net score is what counts for the bet. This is what lets a 20-handicapper genuinely compete with — and beat — a 6 in a fair match.
Allowances for Format

Full, Three-Quarter, or Difference Strokes

Governing-body allowances exist so no format gives too big an edge:

FormatCommon allowance
Singles match playFull difference between the two handicaps
Fourball better-ball85% of course handicap (the standard WHS four-ball better-ball allowance), strokes off the lowest player
Individual Stableford / medal betFull or 95% course handicap

For a purely friendly game you can keep it simple with full difference strokes — but if the group is competitive, using the proper allowances (and the club's own handicap software or the England Golf / WHS app) removes any sense of unfairness.

💡 Practical Tip

Mark the Stroke Holes on the Card

Before you start, everyone circles the holes where they receive a stroke on their scorecard. It takes two minutes on the first tee and prevents every "wait, do I get a shot here?" conversation for the rest of the round.

Etiquette & Settling Up

A money game only stays friendly if everyone follows a few unwritten courtesies. None of them are about golf technique — all of them are about keeping the group happy.

  • 1
    Set a stake everyone is comfortable losing. The right stake is one that focuses the mind but that nobody would mind handing over. If a new or junior player joins, quietly drop the stakes. Never let a bet make anyone anxious about money.
  • 2
    Agree presses and carry-overs in advance. Automatic presses and skin carry-overs can multiply a £2 game into something much bigger. Decide the ceiling before you start.
  • 3
    Keep an honest running count. Nominate one player, or use a notes app, to track the match, skins or points hole by hole. Reconstructing it on the 18th green is where disputes are born.
  • 4
    Settle promptly and gracefully. Pay what you owe at the end of the round without fuss, and collect your winnings the same way. "I'll get you next time" is fine among regulars but should never be assumed.
  • 5
    Know your club's and the law's limits. Keep stakes modest and social. Many clubs have norms (or rules) about betting on the premises, and amateur-status rules limit what a competitive amateur may win — see our Amateur Status Rules guide (Elite tier) if you play in ranked events. When in doubt, keep it to pocket change and pride.
The Spirit of It

Pride Is the Real Currency

The best weekend games are played for stakes small enough that the money is a token and the bragging rights are the real prize. Keep it light, keep it fair, pay up with a smile — and the game will still be running in twenty years.

Get Started This Weekend

Two ready-made games to bring to your next round — one for a pair, one for a fourball.

For Two Players

The £2 Nassau

Agree £2 for the front nine, £2 for the back, £2 for the eighteen. Apply full difference strokes off the lower handicap, circle the stroke holes on the card, and allow one optional press per nine when someone goes two down. Maximum sensible exposure without presses: £6. Simple, classic, and it keeps every hole meaningful.

For a Fourball of Mixed Abilities

Bingo-Bango-Bongo at 10p a Point

No handicap maths required, and it rewards the whole group rather than just the best ball-striker. Three points per hole, 54 points in play across the round, 10p a point. Track it in a phone note. The higher-handicappers stay genuinely competitive because being first on the green and first in the hole matters as much as raw distance.
