Short Game by Lie — Why It Matters

The short game system in Guide 02 covers the fundamentals. This guide goes further: it gives you a specific, reliable technique for every lie type you encounter, so that your decision on the shot is made before you address the ball — not during the swing.

The Most Expensive Lie Type
Tight Lies — The Hidden Handicap

Why Mid-Handicappers Fear the Tight Lie

SG data consistently shows that mid-handicap players (5–12) save significantly fewer shots from tight lies than from rough. This seems counterintuitive — a tight lie should be easier than rough. The reason: tight lies require a shallower attack angle and precise low-point control. Players trained on lush parkland turf develop a steep, digging action that works from rough but skulls or chunks from a tight lie. The cure is deliberate practice from tight lies — which most golfers never do because tight lies are uncomfortable and rough feels safer. The tight lies tab addresses this directly.

Tight Lie — Hardpan, Apron, Bare Ground

A tight lie has no grass cushion between ball and clubface. The common error is too steep an attack angle — the leading edge catches the ground before the ball. The fix is a shallower approach and precise low-point control.

Diagnostic
Is This a Tight Lie?

How to Identify It

  • ?
    The ball sits directly on bare soil, compacted mud, sandy hardpan, or very short tight grass (apron / collar of green, summer-baked fairway).
  • ?
    No grass fibres visible between ball and ground. The ball has no cushion — it is sitting on a hard surface.
  • ?
    If you place your finger horizontally next to the ball, the ball equator is at or below ground level.
Core Technique
The Tight Lie Method

Shallow Arc, Forward Lean, Leading Edge Priority

  • 1
    Club selection: Use less loft than you think. From 15–25 yards on a tight lie, a 9-iron or PW is frequently better than a lob wedge. Less loft = shallower attack angle = less risk of the leading edge digging. Use a lob wedge only if you need to stop the ball quickly with a short carry.
  • 2
    Setup: Ball position middle of stance (not back, not forward). Weight slightly forward — 55%. Hands neutral to very slightly ahead. Do not press the shaft forward excessively — this adds loft back in while increasing the risk of blading.
  • 3
    The key thought: brush the surface. On a tight lie your low point needs to be precisely at the ball — not behind it (chunk) and not above it (skull/thin). Think of brushing the surface rather than hitting down into it. The swing arc must be shallow enough that the club skims the hard surface.
  • 4
    Eyes on the front edge of the ball: Focus your eyes on the front edge (target-side) of the ball rather than the back. This small cognitive shift moves your low point slightly forward and reduces the instinct to hit behind the ball.
  • 5
    Abbreviated backswing, full follow-through: A short, controlled backswing followed by a complete follow-through maintains the arc and prevents deceleration — which on tight lies causes the club to bounce into the ball.

The Most Common Error
Tight Lie Error Pattern

Too Much Loft + Too Steep = Skull or Chunk

The natural instinct on a tight lie is to try to "help" the ball into the air by adding loft and scooping. This produces the opposite — a steep, digging swing arc that either buries the leading edge (chunk) or skips the leading edge across the ball (skull). The fix:

  • !
    Use one less club of loft than feels comfortable.
  • !
    Think "brush the surface" not "hit down."
  • !
    If you chipped fat, the fix is not more weight forward — it is shallower attack angle. More weight forward often makes steep steeper.
📋 Tight Lie Drill — 20 Minutes

The Tee Peg Drill

Place a tee peg in the ground flush with the surface (no height). Place a ball on top of it so the ball sits at exactly ground level. Chip 20 balls in sets of 5 to a target 15–20 yards away. The tee peg forces you to enter at the correct shallow angle — too steep and you snap the peg; too shallow and you contact only air. Track how many shots in each set feel "pure" (zero feedback, clean flight). Target: 4/5 clean contacts before moving to balls on bare ground with no tee.

Rough Lie — Light, Medium, Heavy

Rough is not one lie — it is three, each requiring a different club and technique. The most expensive rough error is playing light-rough technique from heavy rough, or vice versa. Correct classification before club selection.

Light Rough
Light Rough Technique

Similar to Standard Chip — One Adjustment

Light rough gives a slightly cushioned lie that can actually help — the ball launches slightly higher with less spin than from a tight lie. One key adjustment:

  • 1
    Expect less spin and slightly more run. The grass between club and ball reduces spin rate — land the ball slightly shorter than normal and allow more run.
  • 2
    Normal chip technique applies. No major setup change required. Play the shot with your standard chipping motion.
  • 3
    Club selection: Standard club for the distance. The slight flier effect (reduced spin, faster ball speed) is modest from light rough and does not require a significant club change.
Medium Rough
Medium Rough Technique

Open Face, Steep Arc, Commit

  • 1
    Open the clubface 10–15 degrees before gripping: Opening the face before gripping (not rotating the grip) adds effective bounce and prevents the hosel from snagging in the grass.
  • 2
    Steeper attack angle: Ball back of centre, weight more forward (65%). The steeper approach gets through the grass rather than being deflected by it.
  • 3
    Accelerate firmly through impact: Deceleration in medium rough is fatal — the grass absorbs club speed and the ball dies well short. Commit to the shot. A firm, decisive strike through the grass is required.
  • 4
    Expect a "flier" — more run than normal: Medium rough reduces spin. Plan for 20–30% more run than a standard chip. Land shorter and play for the run.
Heavy Rough
Heavy Rough Technique

Survival Mode — Loft, Steepness, Commitment

From heavy rough close to the green, the priority shifts from proximity to getting the ball on the green. Setting a specific proximity target from heavy rough creates unrealistic expectations and hesitation in the swing.

  • 1
    Maximum loft — always a 58° or 60° wedge: Heavy rough requires the most bounce and most loft to get the ball out. No exceptions.
  • 2
    Ball back, face open, wrists firm: Ball well back of centre. Face open 20–25 degrees. Grip pressure firm — heavy rough twists the face at impact; grip firmness maintains face angle.
  • 3
    Very steep, committed swing: Virtually straight down into the back of the ball. The grass is cut through rather than swept through. There is no follow-through in the conventional sense — the club decelerates in the grass.
  • 4
    Manage expectations — "on the green" is success: A save from heavy rough is a bonus. The primary target is a two-putt opportunity anywhere on the green. Proximity is secondary. Do not manufacture expectations that change the swing.

Downslope Chip

The downslope chip is the most technically demanding standard short game shot. The slope delofts the club and produces a low, running trajectory — the opposite of what most players attempt by trying to help the ball into the air.

Why It's Difficult
The Physics of the Downslope

The Ball Will Run — Plan for It

A downslope reduces effective loft by the angle of the slope. A 56° wedge on a 10° downslope has an effective loft of approximately 46°. The ball launches lower and runs further than on flat ground. The instinct to "help" the ball up by hanging back produces a thin, low, running shot that runs through the green. The correct response is to accept the trajectory and manage the landing zone accordingly.

Core Technique
Downslope Method

Match the Slope — Lean Into It

  • 1
    Align shoulders to the slope: The single most important downslope adjustment. Tilt your upper body so your shoulders follow the angle of the ground — lead shoulder down, trail shoulder higher. This aligns your swing arc to the slope rather than fighting it.
  • 2
    Ball position back of centre: Ball further back than normal — roughly below the trail armpit. This ensures the club reaches the ball before it reaches the bottom of the slope.
  • 3
    Weight forward and stay forward: 65–70% weight on lead foot at address. Stay there through the swing. The temptation to hang back and scoop is the primary fault — resist it completely.
  • 4
    Use less loft: The slope delofts your club. A PW or 9-iron on a mild downslope achieves the effective loft of a SW or PW on flat ground. Using a lob wedge on a steep downslope risks the sole bouncing off the slope rather than the leading edge entering the ball.
  • 5
    Land short, play for the run: The ball will run significantly further than on flat ground. Select a landing zone well short of the flag and plan for run-out. On steep downslopes, landing on the fringe or even short of the green is correct — let the ball run to the hole.

📋 Downslope Drill — 20 Minutes

The Slope Confirmation Drill

Find a genuine downslope on the chipping green or fairway edge. Drop 10 balls on the slope. Before each shot, set up with shoulders matching the slope, ball back, weight forward — then deliberately look at where you expect the ball to land and how far it will run. Hit the shot and observe whether the actual outcome matches your prediction. The drill trains your prediction model for downslope shots — after 5–6 sessions your anticipation of run-out from a given slope angle becomes accurate.

Upslope Chip

The upslope chip is the most forgiving non-standard lie in short game. The slope adds effective loft, produces a higher launch, and stops the ball faster. The primary error is over-compensation — using too much loft for a shot that already has excess loft built in by the slope.

Core Technique
Upslope Method

De-Loft, Match the Slope, Commit

  • 1
    Use less loft than you think: A 10° upslope adds approximately 10° of effective loft. A PW on a moderate upslope behaves like a GW. A 58° on an upslope produces a near-vertical shot — useful only for very short distances.
  • 2
    Match shoulders to the slope: As with the downslope, align your shoulder line to the slope — trail shoulder down, lead shoulder up. This prevents the club digging into the upslope at impact.
  • 3
    Ball forward in stance: Ball position slightly forward of centre on upslopes. The bottom of the swing arc is further back than on flat ground; ball forward compensates for this.
  • 4
    Expect less run, more height: The upslope ball stops faster. Plan your landing zone closer to the pin than on flat ground — the ball will not run out as far. This is the advantage of an upslope lie: you can be more aggressive with landing zone selection.
  • 5
    Commit — upslope is forgiving: The higher launch from an upslope means small errors in contact are less costly. The ball lands softer. Make a confident, full-tempo swing — tentative swings produce the only real fault available on an upslope: the sclaff (fat contact into the hill).

On very steep upslopes close to the green, a low-lofted club (7i, 8i) run up the face of the slope and onto the green is frequently the highest-percentage shot — it removes the risk of the steep loft producing too high a trajectory and landing short of the crest.

Bump-and-Run — Close Range System

The bump-and-run is the most underused shot in amateur golf at UK parkland courses and virtually the only shot used by experienced links players from within 30 yards. It removes variables — no loft, no backspin, ground control all the way.

💡

When to choose bump-and-run over a chip: Any time the path from ball to flag is clear of obstacles (no bunker, no significant rough strip, no steep bank). On UK courses this describes the majority of chip shots from around the green — yet most mid-handicap players instinctively reach for loft every time. The bump removes the strike variable and the spin variable simultaneously.

Technique
Bump-and-Run Setup and Stroke

Putting Stroke with a Lofted Club

  • 1
    Narrow stance, ball back: Feet closer than shoulder width. Ball position 3–4cm back of centre. Weight 60% forward.
  • 2
    Hands forward, shaft lean: Hands ahead of ball — 5–10 degrees of shaft lean. This de-lofts the club and promotes a low, skimming contact.
  • 3
    Putting-style arms and shoulders stroke — no wrist break: The bump-and-run is executed exactly like a long putt. Arms and shoulders move together; wrists stay passive throughout. No breaking of the wrists either way.
  • 4
    Target: land on the fairway or fringe — not the green: Select a landing spot on firm, short grass. The ball runs from there. Avoid landing directly on the green surface — the ball arrives too hot and runs past the hole.
  • 5
    Acceleration through impact: Maintain tempo and acceleration through the ball. The deceleration instinct produces a fluffed contact. The swing should be the same length back and through.
📋 Bump-and-Run Drill — 20 Minutes

Distance Ladder

Place three coins or targets at 10, 20, and 30 yards from a fringe area. Using a 7-iron, hit 5 balls to each target in sequence. Then randomise — call your target before hitting. The goal is accurate landing zone selection, not accuracy to the pin. Track how often your ball stops within 3 feet of each target after rolling out. This calibrates your carry-to-run ratio for the specific conditions of the day — which changes with firmness and temperature.

Plugged Lie & Mud Ball

Plugged lies from soft ground and mud balls from winter conditions are survival situations — not scoring opportunities. The correct technique is defined, consistent, and counterintuitive. Follow it precisely.

Plugged Lie — Greenside
Buried Ball — Soft Ground

The Plugged Lie Technique

  • 1
    Close the clubface: The counterintuitive move. A closed face (toe pointing down) prevents the leading edge from bouncing off the compressed soil. An open face — the standard chip setup — will skip over the top of a plugged ball.
  • 2
    Steep, chopping action — straight down: The swing is almost vertical. The club must dig under the plugged ball, not sweep through it. There is no follow-through in the conventional sense — the club decelerates into the soil after extracting the ball.
  • 3
    Expect a low, running ball — no spin: A ball extracted from a plug has almost zero backspin. It comes out low and runs hard. Do not aim at the flag. Plan for significant run-out and target a landing zone well short of the flag — the front third of the green as a maximum.
  • 4
    Success = on the green: A plugged ball on the green is a successful shot. The target is anywhere on the putting surface, preferably the front half. Do not create expectations of proximity that change the swing.
Mud Ball on Approach
Mud Adhering to Ball

Adjusting for Mud — Direction and Distance

  • 1
    Mud on the back of the ball (facing you at address): Ball curves toward the mud. Mud on the left side = ball curves left. Aim right to compensate.
  • 2
    Mud on the front of the ball: Reduces spin and launch angle — ball typically flies lower and shorter. Take an extra half-club and allow for less stopping.
  • 3
    Mud on the top or bottom: Minimal lateral effect but trajectory is altered. Top mud = lower launch; bottom mud = higher launch. Adjust club accordingly.
  • 4
    Cannot predict precisely — aim for the fat part of the green: Mud ball direction is not perfectly predictable even with a known position. Centre-green targeting is always correct on a mud ball approach — do not attack pins.
📜

Rules note: Under preferred lies (winter rules / "lift, clean and place") in force, you may lift a ball, clean mud from it, and place it within the defined distance — typically 6 inches or one club length, no nearer the hole. Always check whether preferred lies are in effect before your round. When in force, mud ball adjustments become irrelevant.

Practice System — SG Tracked by Lie Type

Generic chipping practice does not build lie-specific skill. This system is structured around the six lie types in this guide — one focused session per lie type, with save percentage tracking that feeds into your monthly SG review.

Session Structure
20-Minute Lie-Specific Session

One Session Per Lie Type Per Week

  • 1
    Warm-up (3 min): 10 balls from a standard fairway lie. These are not tracked. They establish feel for the day.
  • 2
    Technique block (10 min): 20 balls from the target lie type, focused entirely on technique — not outcome. Refer to the relevant tab for the specific technique checklist. Note which element of the technique felt cleanest.
  • 3
    Performance block (7 min): 15 balls from the same lie type, tracking save percentage. A save is defined as the chip finishing within the length of a flagstick from the hole (approximately 7 feet). Record: shots hit / saves.
